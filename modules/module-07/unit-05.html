Unit 5

Interviews, Survey Methods, and Questionnaire Design

Learning Outcomes

  • Understand how interviews and surveys fit into research and whether either or both of these might be useful for your investigation.

e-Porfolio Activity: Reflective Activity 2 - Case Study: Inappropriate Use of Surveys

In 2018, Cambridge Analytica was in the news in the United Kingdom and the USA (Confessore, 2018) for obtaining and sharing data obtained from millions of Facebook users. They obtained the data through innocuous surveys on Facebook (you may have seen this type of survey and probably participated at times). This is probably the highest profile of surveys used for alternative means and, probably, monetary gains. However, this happens often through various media.

Consider how exactly this happened and why it was used. Find one or two further examples of inappropriate use of surveys and highlight the impact of all these examples from the various ethical, social, legal and professional standpoints that apply.

Response:

The Cambridge Analytica scandal of 2018 remains one of the most infamous examples of the misuse of surveys. Up to 87 million Facebook users had their personal data harvested via a seemingly innocuous personality quiz app, This Is Your Digital Life, which also captured information about their friends without informed consent (Lapowsky, 2018). Cambridge Analytica leveraged this data for psychographic profiling and targeted political advertising during the 2016 US presidential election and the Brexit referendum, exposing the potent influence such digital profiling can have (Isaak and Hanna, 2018).

A second example involves broader digital platforms, namely TikTok. The Federal Trade Commission revealed in a 2024 report that social media platforms, including TikTok, extensively track user behaviour, collecting sensitive demographic information and sharing it with third parties, often without user knowledge or transparency (The Guardian, 2024). The FTC criticised the companies for weak data minimisation and deletion practices, especially concerning teen users. It concluded that self-regulation fails to protect individuals adequately (The Guardian, 2024).

These two cases share troubling features. From an ethical standpoint, both illustrate violations of trust and autonomy, as surveys and platform interactions were cloaked in seemingly innocuous contexts without transparent consent. The social impact is considerable: Cambridge Analytica eroded confidence in democratic processes, while TikTok’s practices contributed to growing unease about youth privacy, digital surveillance, and manipulation. Legally, Cambridge Analytica triggered substantial regulatory responses, including enforcement actions, fines, and strengthened data protection frameworks. The FTC report reinforces the urgent need for enforceable regulations, given platforms’ longstanding internal resistance to meaningful self-governance. On a professional level, both cases exemplify neglect of ethical data practices, raising questions about accountability and professional integrity in data science, research, and platform governance.

In sum, Cambridge Analytica and the broader tracking practices on platforms like TikTok demonstrate how user data, even when collected under harmless pretexts such as quizzes or app interactions, can be weaponised. They highlight the urgent need for stronger transparency, better legal protections, and for professionals in technology and research to prioritise ethical standards over profit or influence.
