e-Portfolio Activity: Reflective Activity 1 – Ethics in Computing in the age of Generative AI

Read Correa et al. (2023) and Deckard (2023).

From late 2022, generative AI has taken the world by storm, and there is no field of activity that has not been impacted in some way. This is so much truer for Computer Science, which is where it all began. It is important to realise, however, that AI itself is nothing new, per se; and if the renaissance of the field after the ‘winter’ of the 1980s has been slow but constant, today there is the need of a different set of rules.

In the Correa et al (2023) paper, the authors state that “a lot of work is taking place to define the values and ideas that should guide AI advances. A key challenge, however, lies in establishing a consensus on these values, given the diverse perspectives of various stakeholders worldwide and the abstraction of normative discourse. Researchers and policy makers need better tools to catalogue and compare AI governance documents from around the world and to identify points of divergence and commonality.”

After reviewing the article and reading how different countries across the world deal with the generative AI revolution, discuss your views on the subject and recommend what you think could be a suitable course of action. You should justify your stance by also reviewing any papers included in this study or other relevant literature (additional links to industry have been provided as ‘Other Resources’ to the module). Your discussion should also highlight the impact your actions would have on applicable legal, social and professional issues. Please note that there is no right or wrong answer here, this exercise is to help you evaluate the legal, social ethical and professional issues that affect computing professionals in industry.

Response:

Since late 2022, generative AI has been everywhere, from art studios to software labs, changing how we work and create. But AI ethics isn’t brand new. As Deckard (2023) points out, people have cared about fairness, openness and responsibility since the AI winter of the 1980s. What’s new is how fast and how far these models reach, so we need fresh ways to keep AI in check.

In the past few years, dozens of guidelines and principles have popped up, all saying things like “AI should be fair” or “AI must be transparent.” Correa et al. (2023) looked at 200 of these documents and grouped them into 17 big ideas, think transparency, justice and reliability. The problem? Everyone defines these ideas a bit differently. One guide says transparency means “explaining why the AI did what it did,” while another wants “full access to data and code.” That makes it hard for teams to actually follow the rules.

Different places focus on different ideas, too. Correa et al. (2023) found that North America and Europe stress transparency and reliability, but in Asia, there’s extra emphasis on beneficence, making sure AI helps people and avoids harm. EU rules highlight individual rights under GDPR, while China’s guidelines stress social harmony. Deckard (2023) reminds us that ethical AI must fit into real-world contexts and respect local values.

IBM (n.d.) also highlights that trust in AI comes from embedding ethical principles at every stage of the AI lifecycle, from design to deployment, and making sure those principles are governed by both people and processes. Their approach includes fairness, explainability, robustness, transparency, and privacy, all of which help ensure AI is not just powerful, but also responsible.

We need better tools and rules that everyone can use. First, we should build an easy-to-use hub, think a website, that gathers AI guidelines from around the world. Researchers could search by country, principle or type of rule. Correa et al. (2023) already made a prototype in Power BI and Dash, but we should expand it, include more languages and keep it updated.

Second, AI changes fast, so our rules must adapt too. Inspired by how we regulate drugs or cybersecurity, we should test AI models regularly, not just at launch. Public–private partnerships could run these tests, checking fairness, safety and social impact on a rolling basis. Floridi and Cowls (2019) call this “reflexive governance,” where ethics guide every step of the AI lifecycle.

Third, soft guidelines are nice, but companies must follow the rules they have been given. Governments in places like Canada, Germany and the UK (which already have some binding AI laws) should write core ethics principles into law. Imagine “algorithmic impact assessments” as mandatory checks or clear liability rules for AI harms. Professional bodies like BCS can help by certifying AI experts in ethics and making regular training a must.

These steps would have clear effects. Legally, consistent definitions and real laws make it easier for companies to comply and for courts to resolve disputes, whether about bias or copyright. Socially, public reports on AI testing, like financial disclosures, let everyone see how safe, fair and transparent these systems are. Communities can hold developers accountable. Professionally, AI practitioners will need new skills in ethics, law and social science. Official certifications from BCS or similar bodies would recognise that expertise and encourage ongoing learning.

To sum up, we don’t need to fear or ignore generative AI. With the right mix of open tools, flexible governance and binding rules, we can enjoy AI’s benefits while keeping it responsible and fair. After all, ethics is the solid ground that makes trustworthy AI possible.

References:

Correa, N.K. et al. (2023) ‘Worldwide AI ethics: A review of 200 guidelines and recommendations for AI governance’, Patterns, 4(10). Available at: https://doi.org/10.1016/j.patter.2023.100857

Deckard, R. (2023) What are ethics in AI?. Available at: https://www.bcs.org/articles-opinion-and-research/what-are-ethics-in-ai/ (Accessed: 3 August 2025).

Floridi, L., and Cowls, J. (2019) ‘A Unified Framework of Five Principles for AI in Society’, Harvard Data Science Review, 1(1). Available at: https://doi.org/10.1162/99608f92.8cd550d1

IBM (n.d.) What is AI Ethics?. Available at: https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/ai-ethics (Accessed: 3 August 2025).
